# Hevner et al. 2004

## Bibliographic Info
- **Authors:** Alan R. Hevner, Salvatore T. March, Jinsoo Park, Sudha Ram
- **Year:** 2004
- **Title:** Design Science in Information Systems Research
- **Venue:** MIS Quarterly, 28(1), 75-105
- **URL:** https://aisel.aisnet.org/misq/vol28/iss1/6/

## Summary
Seven guidelines for conducting and evaluating design science research: (1) design as artifact, (2) problem relevance, (3) design evaluation, (4) research contributions, (5) research rigor, (6) design as search process, (7) communication. Over 20,000 citations. The closest thing to a "CONSORT for design science."

## Key Findings
- DSR outputs: constructs, models, methods, instantiations (per March & Smith 1995)
- Design principles are prescriptive ("in context C, use mechanism M"), not descriptive claims
- Evaluation evidence is required but the artifact itself IS the contribution
- Explicitly cautioned against using guidelines as "mindless checklists"
- DSR requires both rigor (research methods) and relevance (practical importance)

## Relevance to DR-004
Design science papers have a fundamentally different atomic unit: the design principle (prescriptive) rather than the claim (descriptive). Verification shifts from "is this true?" to "is this well-grounded in justificatory knowledge and does evaluation evidence support it?" The seven guidelines provide a natural verification checklist for design science papers -- analogous to the claim registry's priority/confidence/source structure but for design artifacts.
