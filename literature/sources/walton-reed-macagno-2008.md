# Walton, Reed & Macagno 2008

## Bibliographic Info
- **Authors:** Douglas Walton, Christopher Reed, Fabrizio Macagno
- **Year:** 2008
- **Title:** Argumentation Schemes
- **Publisher:** Cambridge University Press
- **ISBN:** 978-0-521-72374-9
- **DOI:** 10.1017/CBO9780511802034

## Summary
Comprehensive compendium of 96 argumentation schemes -- templates representing common types of defeasible (presumptive) arguments. Each scheme has a pattern (premise-to-conclusion structure) and a set of critical questions that, if not satisfactorily answered, defeat the argument. The critical questions serve as ready-made verification checklists.

## Key Findings
- 96 schemes organized into deductive, inductive, and defeasible/presumptive categories
- Most common in academic writing: argument from expert opinion, analogy, cause-to-effect, sign, example, practical reasoning
- Each scheme comes with critical questions as verification items
- For "argument from expert opinion" (citation-based claims): 6 critical questions covering credibility, field expertise, reliability, consistency with other experts, evidence basis
- Defeasibility aligns with confidence tiers -- all presumptive arguments are defeatable

## Relevance to DR-004
The most directly operationalizable model for verification infrastructure. Critical questions are ready-made checklists per argument type. For a working subset, 6-7 schemes cover the vast majority of academic paper arguments. The "argument from expert opinion" scheme directly models citation-based claims -- the most common pattern. Extended the claim registry could include a "scheme" field identifying which argumentation pattern is being used, with associated critical questions as verification items.
