# Gregor 2006

## Bibliographic Info
- **Authors:** Shirley Gregor
- **Year:** 2006
- **Title:** The Nature of Theory in Information Systems
- **Venue:** MIS Quarterly, 30(3), 611-642
- **URL:** https://aisel.aisnet.org/misq/vol30/iss3/5/

## Summary
Proposes five types of theory based on four goals (analysis, explanation, prediction, prescription). Legitimizes Type I (analytic/taxonomic) and Type V (design) contributions as genuine theory -- both non-empirical paper types that don't fit claim-source verification cleanly.

## Key Findings
- Type I: Analytic -- says "what is" (frameworks, taxonomies, ontologies)
- Type II: Explanatory -- says "how" and "why"
- Type III: Predictive -- says "what will be"
- Type IV: Explanatory & Predictive -- most traditional social science theories
- Type V: Design & Action -- says "how to do something" (design principles, methods)
- Types I and V are non-empirical; their contributions cannot be verified against sources because they ARE the new contribution

## Relevance to DR-004
Provides the theoretical justification for why the claim registry model doesn't cover all paper types. The five-type taxonomy can guide which verification approach applies: Types II-IV fit the claim registry well (empirical/explanatory claims). Types I and V need different atomic units -- constructs and relationships (Type I) or design principles and evaluation evidence (Type V).
