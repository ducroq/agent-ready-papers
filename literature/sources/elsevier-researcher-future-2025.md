# Elsevier "Researcher of the Future" (2025)

## Bibliographic Info
- **Organization:** Elsevier (Confidence in Research series)
- **Year:** 2025
- **Title:** Researcher of the Future — a Confidence in Research report
- **URL:** https://www.elsevier.com/insights/confidence-in-research/researcher-of-the-future
- **DOI:** none (industry report; PDF, infographic, and databook downloadable from the landing page)
- **Verified:** 2026-06-12 — statistics below read directly from the report landing page, not from press coverage

## Summary
Elsevier survey of 3,200+ researchers across 113 countries on AI adoption, trust, and research-integrity attitudes. Headline finding is a large adoption–trust gap: 84% of researchers have used AI tools, but only 22% believe AI tools are currently trustworthy. This is the survey behind Elsevier's marketing for LeapSpace (their AI research workspace, launched November 2025).

## Key Findings
- "84% of researchers have used AI tools for work or other purposes"
- "Only 22% of respondents believe AI tools are currently trustworthy"
- "58% of researchers now use AI tools in their work, up from 37% in 2024"
- 61% use AI for finding/summarizing research; 51% for literature reviews
- Only 27% believe they have adequate AI training; 32% say their institution provides good AI governance
- "74% believe peer-reviewed research remains trustworthy and essential to research integrity"
- 68% say publish-or-perish pressure has intensified

## Caveats (tier discipline)
- **Industry self-published, not peer-reviewed.** Elsevier has a direct commercial stake: LeapSpace is sold as the answer to the trust gap this survey reports. Usable as attributed descriptive evidence ("an Elsevier survey of 3,200+ researchers reports..."), not as SUPPORTED-tier evidence of researcher attitudes in general.
- **Methodology partially disclosed on the landing page** (n, country count, year); fieldwork dates and sampling frame require the downloadable PDF/databook — check before load-bearing use.
- **Conflation risk:** distinct from Elsevier's *Insights 2024: Attitudes toward AI* (3,000 researchers + clinicians, 123 countries, different figures). Press coverage mixes the two.
- **Exact wording matters for the 22%:** respondents who "believe AI tools are currently trustworthy" — a statement about the tools, not about willingness to use them (the 84%/58% show use despite distrust).

## Relevance to Paper 1 (The Verification Gap)
Direct quantitative support for the paper's central framing: researchers have adopted AI far faster than they trust it, and nothing in the adoption workflow closes that gap. The 84%-use / 22%-trust spread is the gap the framework's verification infrastructure addresses from the author side; Elsevier's LeapSpace (Trust Cards, Claim Radar) addresses the same gap from the literature-consumption side — independent commercial confirmation that claim-level verification is the recognized failure mode, while the production side (verifying claims in one's own manuscript) remains unaddressed by publisher tooling.
